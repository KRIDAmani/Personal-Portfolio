The Observer

Photography taught me to slow down and really look at a scene before reaching for the camera. Engineering turned out to ask the same thing: take the time to understand the problem, and the right approach usually becomes clear.

My Story

I followed the questions.

I never really picked one specialization. I just kept asking the same question: how does this actually work?

It started with wanting to understand how things work under the hood, not just use them: why a ranking model prefers one answer over another, why a network drops a packet, why a robot arm hesitates for a fraction of a second before it moves. Whether it was AI, security, robotics or distributed systems, I kept finding the same question underneath.

That same habit is what got me into photography. A street at night is just light and shapes until you decide what actually matters in the frame. I approach a messy dataset or a vague spec the same way: look carefully, cut what doesn't matter, then commit to a direction.

I'd rather not be pigeonholed as just "the AI person." I want to be the kind of engineer who looks for the right tool for the problem, and who can explain that choice clearly, whether to another engineer or to someone who's never written a line of code.
